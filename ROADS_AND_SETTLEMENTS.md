# Luo Kai: Open World — Roads and Settlements

## Purpose
Make travel feel like moving through a real civilization, not hopping between points on a map.

## Road hierarchy
### 1. Village lanes
- short, practical, narrow
- connect houses, storage, shrine, and edge gate
- frequent foot traffic
- lanterns and hand-made markers

### 2. Trade roads
- wider, packed dirt or stone where wealthy enough
- connect settlements, camps, and markets
- used by merchants, hunters, and messengers
- visible wear from carts and repeated travel

### 3. Patrol roads
- roads with guard presence or sect oversight
- safer, better maintained
- may include watch posts, bells, and sign stones
- more frequent at dawn and dusk

### 4. Wild tracks
- narrow, less maintained routes through forest or ridge
- used by hunters, smugglers, and experienced travelers
- often partially hidden
- weather can erase or reveal them

### 5. Ritual paths
- symbolic routes to shrines, ancestral places, or mountain sites
- marked with stones, banners, incense holders, or talismans
- not the fastest route, but spiritually meaningful

## Settlement types

### Village edge settlement
- where the player starts
- food, shelter, storage, and small trade
- closer to the forest than the sect
- believable because it depends on the land around it

### Road post
- small structure for rest, information, and travel safety
- may include a lamp, bench, notice board, and water barrel
- one or two NPCs at most
- exists because travel needs places to pause

### Shrine settlement
- less about commerce, more about spiritual balance and ritual
- offers incense, prayer, and qi-related requests
- often built near a landmark or river crossing

### Training camp
- body cultivation and discipline focused
- open yard, racks, practice posts, and simple shelters
- often near cliffs, clearings, or sect-adjacent space

### Sect outpost
- guarded, formal, and not fully welcoming
- controls access to better cultivation resources
- usually on higher ground or near a strategic road junction

## Settlement layout rules
- people sleep somewhere believable
- work happens near resources
- storage is near roads or water
- shrines are placed where the land feels spiritually important
- roads enter and leave settlements in ways that imply trade and defense
- no settlement should be symmetrical just for looks

## Settlement growth over time
As the player progresses:
- some paths get better maintained
- some camps become larger or more permanent
- some guards appear where danger used to be
- some settlements open new gates or close old unsafe routes
- some vendors move to better locations if travel becomes safer

## Signs of life
A settlement feels alive when the player can see:
- smoke from cooking fires
- washed clothes or drying herbs
- stacked crates or baskets
- tools left near a work area
- children or errand runners in safe areas
- guards at posts
- lamps or incense at dusk

## Settlement story logic
Every settlement should answer:
1. why do people live here?
2. what do they need from the land?
3. what are they afraid of?
4. who controls the roads?
5. what changes when the player helps them?
