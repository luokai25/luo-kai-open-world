# Luo Kai: Open World — Travel and Routes

## Purpose
Define how the world is connected so movement feels like a journey, not a menu hop.

## Route philosophy
Every route should answer one of these questions:
- how do people move?
- why do they risk this path?
- what place does it connect?
- what is hidden off it?
- what danger makes it meaningful?

## Main route types
### 1. Village roads
- safest, most used, broadest paths
- connect homes, shrine, market, and fields
- lined with markers, lanterns, or fences

### 2. Forest trails
- narrower and more uncertain
- useful for gathering and hidden exploration
- often split into safe and risky branches

### 3. Bridge crossings
- important transitions and chokepoints
- often tied to requests, danger, or memory
- visually memorable from afar

### 4. Ridge switchbacks
- slow, exposed, vertical travel
- create anticipation for the next region
- useful for making scale feel real

### 5. Sect approach roads
- more formal and controlled
- marked by discipline, barriers, and ritual geometry

## Travel pacing
Travel should have a rhythm.
- easy ground first
- visible landmark second
- decision point third
- danger or reward fourth
- destination reveal last

## Travel experience changes
Travel should feel different by context.
- day vs night
- clear vs fog
- safe vs hunted
- low realm vs high realm
- local route vs long route

## Checkpoints and rest pockets
Routes should contain believable places to pause.
- roadside camp
- shrine bench
- watch post
- river crossing rest space
- cliff shelf overlook

## Hidden route design
Hidden routes should feel like discoveries, not collectibles.
- a path behind roots
- a break in the fence
- a side slope above a bridge
- a creek bed that leads somewhere useful
- a trail visible only from a higher point

## Return route logic
Once the player advances, returning should reveal the world differently.
- what was dangerous becomes easy
- what was hidden becomes obvious
- NPC reactions change
- the player notices what they missed before

## Why it matters
If routes feel real, the world feels explorable. If routes are just links between tasks, the world feels fake.
